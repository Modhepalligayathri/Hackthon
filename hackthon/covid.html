You may be wondering — do I still have the antibodies? Will they last forever? Am I truly “immune” to the virus, as former president Donald Trump suggested?

According to the NIH Director’s blog, COVID antibodies could last up to three or four months after infection. No one knows yet how long vaccine-induced immunity may last, but estimates range from months to years. Some experts believe that booster shots may be necessary in the future.

Even if you’ve gotten sick already, experts recommend getting the vaccine. But if you were treated with monoclonal antibodies or convalescent plasma while you were sick, the CDC advises you should wait 90 days before getting vaccinated.

You decide to get a COVID-19 vaccine.

Medical professionals are trying to spread the word that COVID-19 vaccines are safe. Though these drugs went through an expedited approval process, that does not mean they’re rushed.

“The mRNA vaccine, while it’s a newer type of vaccine, has really been under development for five or 10 years,” said Dr. Martin Siglin of AMITA Health at a “Coronavirus Q&A” webinar held by the city of Evanston, Ill. “There’s been a lot of work going on behind the scenes that was used with Ebola and others, so it’s really been in preparation for quite a long time.”

The vaccines fast-tracked approval because various stages of trials, which are usually done one after another, were done at the same time, said Dr. Kartik Cherabuddi, an infectious disease specialist at the University of Florida.

Plus, trials are now complemented by millions of people who have received vaccinations outside of an experimental setting.

“We've seen many, many, many people vaccinated now, even in real world settings, and it's found to be quite safe,” Cherabuddi said. “Severe reactions are very few, in sort of the order of two to three per million vaccinations. That’s very low when we compare it to anything else — a road traffic accident is more likely than a severe reaction to the vaccine.”

The safety of the vaccines still needs to be tested for children, though.

The Pfizer-BioNTech COVID-19 vaccine

Date approved by FDA: Dec. 11, 2020

Number of doses: 2

Days between doses: 21

Percent efficacy in trials: 95%

Type of vaccine: mRNA

The COVID-19 vaccines approved so far in the U.S., including the Pfizer-BioNTech vaccine, are mRNA vaccines. They do not put the coronavirus, in any form, into your body. Instead, these vaccines give your cells instructions on how to fight the COVID-19 coronavirus if you ever do come into contact with it.

mRNA vaccines teach your cells to make a kind of protein called the “spike protein.” When your immune system notices these proteins, it starts making antibodies to protect you, even though the protein your cells have made is actually harmless. But because your body learns to protect you against the version of the spike protein your cells made, your immune system will likely be able to also protect you from the spike protein on the COVID-19 virus.

Pfizer and Moderna are the companies with mRNA COVID-19 vaccines approved in the United States. But the differences between them are subtle.

"When you start sub-analyzing the studies [or] try to... find differences, I think you would find them really not as significant,” Cherabuddi said. “Bottom line, I think they're the same. Persons who want to get immunized shouldn’t wait for one versus the other. Whatever is offered to them first would be my recommendation to take."

Other vaccines

AstraZeneca

Date approved by FDA: Not approved yet in the U.S.

Number of doses: 2

Days between doses: Up to 12 weeks

Percent efficacy in trials: 70.4%

Type of vaccine: DNA

The AstraZeneca vaccine uses DNA, which is different from the mRNA vaccines like Pfizer and Moderna that are currently approved in the U.S. DNA vaccines put a gene from COVID-19's spike protein into another kind of virus called an adenovirus, modified so that it can't replicate.

The adenovirus is able to enter your cells, which can read the gene and use mRNA to make copies of the spike protein. The adenovirus sparks your immune system alarm response, and your body begins to build antibodies to destroy cells with the spike protein.

In the United States, the Johnson & Johnson vaccine uses DNA, and it is only one dose. The Pfizer and Moderna vaccines both use mRNA.

Ultimately, it doesn't really matter which vaccine you get, Cherabuddi said. They all do essentially the same thing.

"Persons who want to get immunized shouldn’t wait for one versus the other," Cherabuddi said. "Whatever is offered to them first would be my recommendation to take."

NONE OF THESE?

You are pregnant.

There are plenty of vaccines that are safe to take while pregnant. The tetanus, diphtheria and whooping cough vaccine, for example, is actually recommended for pregnant people to take. But there hasn't yet been much research on the safety or efficacy of the COVID-19 vaccine for people who are pregnant.

mRNA vaccines do not contain a live virus, so they can't give you or your baby COVID-19. Limited data from animal studies show no safety concerns in rats that received the Moderna vaccine.

Human studies are just beginning: On February 18, Pfizer and BioNTech announced that they started a study with 4,000 volunteers to measure the vaccine's effect on pregnant women. Based on the general public's experience, the vaccine appears to be safe for people who are pregnant, but studies should help determine that.

Some new research suggests that getting the vaccine while pregnant may cause antibodies to cross the placenta and likely help protect the child.

According to the CDC, getting vaccinated while pregnant is a "personal choice" for those who belong to a group eligible to get the vaccine. If you have questions about it, talk to your healthcare provider.

Spare vaccine doses

There have been tales told online of people waiting at vaccination sites to receive a COVID-19 vaccine before they're eligible to. At the end of the day, these sites might have extra opened vaccines that would get thrown out if they're not used immediately.

If an appointment is cancelled, for example, there could be leftover doses at the end of the day.

Cherabuddi said that some places have a back-up list of who to call if there are extra vaccines. Others might allow you to sign up to be on that list.

"Even if you're not in high priority groups, if your location close to you enables you to sign up for something like that, please do," Cherabuddi said. "Rather than throwing it in the bin, it's better to get it in the arm of someone."

Want a vaccine?

Almost two million people have created profiles on the website Dr. B, which connects people with spare vaccine doses. If there are extra vaccinations available in your area, you may be contacted to get a shot. You just have to respond yes, and get to the vaccination site within a certain time frame.

You decide to leave the vaccination site without waiting.

This could be risky. You leave the vaccination site, and luckily, you don't experience an extreme allergic reaction to the vaccine. You do, however, experience soreness on your arm and a bit of swelling. These are common side effects of the vaccine. You can ease the discomfort with a cold, wet washcloth or by using or exercising your arm.

Still, leaving the vaccination site before the recommended time could be dangerous if you do experience adverse effects, as you would no longer have a doctor around.

TRY AGAIN?

You decide to wait the recommended time.

You wait the allotted 15 minutes, and you feel fine. But later that day, when you get home, you start to feel a little off. Since you didn’t experience severe effects in the first hour after vaccination, you should be okay. But you do feel a bit like you have the flu.

"These are... your immune system reactions," Cherabuddi said. "The most common ones are sort of a flu-like illness, fevers, body pains, joint pains, chills."

Though these side effects are "fairly common," Cherabuddi said, they go away within 24 to 36 hours.

To George Washington University medical student CJ Patel, who got the first dose of the Pfizer vaccine on Jan. 23, side effects wouldn't deter him from getting vaccinated.

"The vaccine's around 95% effective," Patel said. "So I think that having one day of very minimal symptoms that are similar to a very light flu or cold is totally, totally worth it."

Patel, for one, did not experience side effects with the first dose of the Pfizer vaccine. His arm felt sore, as it typically does after getting vaccinated. But in this case, Patel said he felt less sore than he usually does after getting a flu shot.

TRY AGAIN?

Even though you are vaccinated, you still use caution, social distance and wear a mask.

Even though the COVID-19 vaccine protects you against severe cases of illness, studies haven't yet proven that it prevents you from carrying the virus.

This means that even after getting the vaccine, you could be an asymptomatic carrier of the COVID-19 virus. You would be protected from its effects, but you could still pass it on to others.

That's why it's important to keep wearing a mask and distancing for a while after getting the shot.

"We know the vaccine — and with a very good efficacy, in general — prevents more symptomatic and severe illness," Cherabuddi said. "Does it prevent a very mild or asymptomatic illness? We don't know that."

However, recent guidance from the CDC gave the green light for holding indoor maskless gatherings with friends who have also been vaccinated. While distancing and wearing masks in public is still necessary, you could ease restrictions while around other vaccinated people.

Though the vaccine may not prevent transmission, experts urge that everyone be vaccinated. There are still benefits to protecting yourself against COVID-19.

"We want to make sure we don't die,” Cherabuddi said. “Getting the vaccine prevents you from getting the more severe symptoms, hospitalization and death."

The COVID-19 vaccines are a light at the end of the tunnel.

But the timeline to get there is still unclear. Cherabuddi said that 70 to 80 percent of the population must be vaccinated before we hit herd immunity. We don’t know when that will be, but some important milestones are in the works.

On Feb. 11, the federal government sent about 1 million vaccines to retail pharmacies across the country. And at the end of that month, the FDA approved the one-dose Johnson & Johnson vaccine.

President Joe Biden recently projected that the United States will have enough vaccines for all adults by mid-May. Still, it will take longer for everyone to actually receive those vaccines.

And masks are going to be a staple in pandemic fashion for months to come, even after the vaccines are widely available. It's possible that masks may be mainstream through 2022, said Dr. Anthony Fauci, director of the National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases.

There’s still a long way to go toward pandemic control and recovery, but the COVID-19 vaccines are currently the best tool we have to get there.

“I know there's a lot of uncertainty in the air,” medical student Patel said. “But I think it's so important to ... make sure you're doing your part, getting the vaccine.”
